Sleep Disturbances in Menopause — Knowledge Base

1. Causes
Q: Why is sleep harder during menopause?
A: Estrogen and progesterone decline disrupt the hypothalamus, which controls sleep and temperature. This leads to night sweats, insomnia, and lighter sleep.

2. Morning Sunlight
Q: Why should I get morning sunlight?
A: It resets circadian rhythm, boosts cortisol for energy, and helps melatonin production for sleep at night.
Q: How much do I need?
A: Aim for 10–30 minutes within an hour of waking, even on cloudy days.

3. Evening Light Hygiene
Q: Why avoid screens at night?
A: Blue light suppresses melatonin, delaying sleep. Use amber lighting, dim lamps, or blue-light filters after sunset.

4. Bedroom Environment
Q: What’s the best bedroom setup?
A: Keep your room cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C), dark, and quiet. Use breathable bedding, blackout curtains, or white-noise machines.

5. Lifestyle & Bedtime Routine
Q: How can I improve sleep naturally?
A: Create a calming wind-down routine (reading, journaling, stretching), avoid screens an hour before bed, and keep consistent sleep/wake times.

6. Nutrition & Supplements
Q: What should I avoid before bed?
A: Large meals, spicy foods, alcohol, or refined carbs within 3 hours of bedtime.
Q: What foods support sleep?
A: Fatty fish, nuts, leafy greens, and kiwi.
Q: What supplements may help?
A: Magnesium, tart cherry juice, or omega-3s. Always check with your doctor.

7. Exercise & Relaxation
Q: When is the best time to exercise?
A: Morning or early afternoon supports sleep. Avoid intense workouts 3–4 hours before bed.
Q: Can I exercise at night?
A: Yes, but keep it light—gentle stretching, yoga, or walking are best.

8. Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR)
Q: What is NSDR?
A: Practices like Yoga Nidra with breathwork and body scans that mimic restorative sleep.
Q: How does it help?
A: It lowers cortisol, calms the nervous system, and prepares the body for sleep.

9. Consistent Sleep Routine
Q: Why is consistency important?
A: Regular bed/wake times anchor circadian rhythm.
Q: When should I stop caffeine?
A: After noon—caffeine lingers for 6–8 hours.

10. Medical Help
Q: What if lifestyle changes don’t work?
A: Talk to a menopause specialist about HRT, which may reduce night sweats and improve sleep by 50–70%.
Q: Are there non-hormonal medications?
A: Yes. Options include fezolinetant, gabapentin, or low-dose SSRIs.
Q: Can therapy help?
A: Yes. CBT-I improves chronic insomnia by 40–60%.

11. Additional Tips
Q: Should I track my sleep?
A: Yes. Journals or wearables can reveal triggers.
Q: Can stress management help?
A: Absolutely—mindfulness, NSDR, and breathwork lower cortisol.
Q: Does community support help?
A: Yes. Peer support reduces isolation and improves consistency.
