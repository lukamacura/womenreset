1. Causes: Why GSM Happens
Subtopics & Q&A
Estrogen Decline
Q: What causes GSM?
A: GSM is mainly caused by the drop in estrogen during perimenopause and menopause. Estrogen keeps vaginal and urinary tissues elastic, moist, and healthy.
Tissue Changes
Q: What happens to my tissues without estrogen?
A: The vaginal walls become thinner, drier, and more sensitive. Natural acidity decreases, making infections more likely.
Collagen & Elasticity
Q: Why do tissues feel fragile?
A: Estrogen supports collagen. When it declines, tissues lose flexibility, leading to dryness and small tears.
Urinary Impact
Q: Can GSM affect the bladder?
A: Yes. Low estrogen weakens the bladder and urethra, which may cause urgency, leakage, or infections.
Risk Factors
Q: What increases the risk?
A: Smoking, certain medications (like anti-estrogens), premature menopause, and ignoring early symptoms like dryness.

2. Symptoms: How GSM Shows Up
Vaginal & Genital Symptoms
Q: What are common vaginal symptoms?
A: Dryness, irritation, pain during sex, spotting, and changes in discharge.
Urinary Symptoms
Q: What urinary symptoms occur?
A: Frequent UTIs, urgency, leakage, burning with urination, and waking up at night to urinate.
Emotional Impact
Q: Can GSM affect relationships?
A: Yes. Painful sex or frequent symptoms can lower libido, increase anxiety, and strain intimacy.

3. Solutions: What Can Help
Lifestyle & Non-Hormonal (first-line)
Q: What can I try without hormones?
A: Vaginal moisturizers, lubricants for sex, gentle hygiene, breathable underwear, hydration, and regular sexual activity.
Q: Can exercise help?
A: Yes. Pelvic floor therapy improves circulation, muscle tone, and bladder support.
Q: Do supplements work?
A: Omega-3s, vitamin E, and soy may help mildly, but focus on anti-inflammatory foods.
